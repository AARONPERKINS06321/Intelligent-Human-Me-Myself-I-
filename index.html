About the Framework

Comprehensive Analysis: Dr. Amy J. Baker's 17 Strategies

This section provides a meticulous examination of each of Dr. Amy J. Baker's 17 primary parental alienation strategies, detailing the precise definitions, the number of verified factual events, and highlighting the most egregious documented instance.

1. Badmouthing the Targeted Parent

Definition: "The alienating parent uses verbal and non-verbal communication to convey that the targeted parent is unloving, unsafe, and unavailable. They exaggerate existing flaws and invent non-existent ones, making frequent, intense, and sincere negative statements without any positive balance."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "The children also explicitly called the targeted parent a "wife beater," refusing contact and accusing them of violence, despite no prior history or evidence of such behavior, and the alienating parent having privately admitted these claims were untrue. Most recently, one child asked, "if I beat the kitty, he said or just mom," further extending the narrative of abuse to include pets."

2. Limiting Contact with the Targeted Parent

Definition: "The alienating parent violates parenting plans or exploits ambiguities to maximize their time with the child. This reduces the targeted parent's opportunities to counteract the badmouthing, leading to a weakening of the parent-child attachment. The child becomes accustomed to less time with the targeted parent, and courts may even formalize this new "status quo"."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Additionally, evidence from the children’s messages shows contact was actively curtailed: for example, a child texted the targeted parent, “Dad we love you and are safe. The constant messaging from you is giving me anxiety. I’m going to block you for the time being... Please stop.” This unusual directive from a child strongly implies that the alienating parent supported or instructed such limitation of contact."

3. Interfering with Communication

Definition: "The alienating parent demands constant access to the child when the child is with the targeted parent but does not reciprocate. They may not answer phones, block emails, or fail to forward messages, limiting the targeted parent's ability to be part of the child's daily life and share small moments."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "The targeted parent explicitly states “there’s no way I can get in touch with you at all” regarding both the alienating parent and the children. It’s also documented that even via the court-sanctioned co-parenting app, the alienating parent read messages from the targeted parent but chose not to reply."

4. Interfering with Symbolic Communication

Definition: "The alienating parent creates an environment where the child feels unable to think about, talk about, or look at pictures of the targeted parent. Conversely, the alienating parent can maintain their presence even when the child is with the targeted parent, leading the child to be preoccupied with thoughts of the alienating parent, making frequent calls, following their rules, and worrying about their disapproval. This preoccupation leaves no room for the child's thoughts and feelings about the targeted parent."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Furthermore, there is consistent and documented evidence, including video recordings, that the children are in constant text contact with an unknown third party (likely the alienating parent or maternal family) throughout their time with the targeted parent. This behavior has been observed repeatedly, despite the targeted parent’s attempts to make them stop. The children also explicitly refused to take pictures with the targeted parent, and they deny any expression of connection."

5. Withdrawal of Love

Definition: "Alienating parents make their approval paramount to the child, causing the child to fear disappointing or angering them. Often, what angers the alienating parent most is the child's affection for the targeted parent. To secure one parent's love, the child must give up the love of the other, a dynamic that may not be explicit to the child but is evident to the targeted parent."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Crucially, the children now refuse to hug, touch, or engage with the targeted parent affectionately; all physical and emotional affectionate contact has ceased entirely post-separation. During a recent visit, when the targeted parent inadvertently made physical contact with one child, the child responded by striking them, then immediately apologized, demonstrating an extreme aversion to physical contact."

6. Telling the Child that the Targeted Parent is Dangerous

Definition: "This is a specific form of badmouthing where the alienating parent creates the impression that the targeted parent is or has been dangerous. They may tell stories of harm, which the child, despite having no memory of them, will believe, especially if repeated frequently."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "The children also directly called the targeted parent a "wife beater" and "abuser," displaying overt fear and avoidance despite no evidence or prior concern. These accusations only arose post-separation and post-removal from the targeted parent's home. Most recently, one son asked if the targeted parent "beat the kitty, he said or just mom," explicitly extending the narrative of danger and abuse to a vulnerable pet, further cementing the portrayal of the targeted parent as a threat."

7. Forcing Child to Choose

Definition: "The alienating parent exploits ambiguities in parenting plans and creates situations to compel the child away from the targeted parent. This includes scheduling competing activities or promising valued items and privileges. If both parents are present, the child will favor the alienating parent and may ignore or be rude to the targeted parent."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "The children now explicitly demand loyalty to the alienating parent, refuse to communicate with the targeted parent, and will not visit or interact unless physically compelled. They have also explicitly asked to leave visitation early, stating they would be "more comfortable at home." During a recent visit, one child asked if parenting time could be changed to a one-hour visit (weekly or biweekly) at a restaurant, indicating a strong desire to limit and control interaction, consistent with a forced choice."

8. Telling the Child that the Targeted Parent Does not Love Him or Her

Definition: "Another form of badmouthing, this involves the alienating parent allowing or encouraging the child to believe the targeted parent does not love them. They might link the end of the marriage to the end of the parent's love for the child (e.g., "Mommy left us" or "Daddy does not love us anymore"). The alienating parent fosters a belief of rejection by the targeted parent, distorting situations to support this narrative."

Status: Partially Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Limited documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "The targeted parent, in an email, expressed concern about “what the children are hearing” and emphasized that “they deserve to know they are loved by both parents.” This statement strongly implies that the targeted parent feared the children were being led to believe they no longer cared, possibly by the alienating parent framing the targeted parent's move from the family home as them "leaving" them."

9. Confiding in the Child

Definition: "The alienating parent involves the child in legal discussions and shares private information about the targeted parent that the child does not need to know. They portray themselves as a victim, inducing pity and protectiveness from the child towards them, and anger and hurt towards the targeted parent. These confidences are shared to flatter the child and appeal to their desire to be trusted in adult matters."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Furthermore, the children’s direct statements like "we will see you in court" and their constant questioning of why the targeted parent follows the agreement when it's "not helpful for them" demonstrate their inappropriate involvement in legal and financial discussions that should be reserved for adults, clearly indicating confiding behavior. During a recent visit, the children also raised questions about past family medical decisions and household issues, indicating their continued exposure to adult disputes."

10. Forcing the Child to Reject the Targeted Parent

Definition: "Alienating parents create scenarios where the child actively rejects the targeted parent, such as having the child cancel parenting time or request the targeted parent not attend important events. This denies the targeted parent their desires and delivers the news via the child, causing hurt and frustration. The targeted parent's potential lashing out further damages the fragile relationship, and once the child has hurt a parent, the alienation becomes entrenched as the child justifies their behavior by devaluing the targeted parent."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Most recently, the children have refused to hug or let the targeted parent touch them, and completely refuse to talk to or listen to them in any way at all. They also explicitly asked to leave the visitation early, stating they would be "more comfortable at home," with the oldest child even saying they would "rather be in prison then here at visitations with me." These behaviors represent an active and comprehensive rejection of the targeted parent, strongly indicative of being forced or encouraged to do so."

11. Asking the Child to Spy on the Targeted Parent

Definition: "The alienating parent may directly or indirectly suggest that the targeted parent has information (e.g., paystubs, legal documents) that they are withholding. They might incentivize the child by linking the information to the child's desires (e.g., "if we knew Daddy got a raise, we could buy you a new dog"). Betraying a parent by spying can lead to guilt and discomfort, further contributing to the alienation."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Yes. During a recorded visit, all three children asked the targeted parent for permission to look through their emails, search their apartment for anything illegal, look through their phone, and search through their drawers. The children were polite but persistent and repeatedly asked if they could check the place for “anything.” This entire exchange was captured on camera by the targeted parent as direct evidence."

12. Asking the Child to Keep Secrets from the Targeted Parent

Definition: "The alienating parent asks or hints that certain information should be withheld from the targeted parent to protect the child's interests. For example, "If Mommy knew we were planning a trip, she'd try to stop it. Let's not tell her until Saturday." Similar to spying, keeping secrets creates psychological distance and discomfort for the child with the targeted parent, and the targeted parent will be hurt or angry upon discovering the withheld information."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "One child asked the targeted parent, “Where are you?” and the only response given was, “Safe.” They would not disclose their location. This suggests that the child had been instructed not to tell the targeted parent their whereabouts. It was a calculated reassurance that gave no actual information, a textbook example of keeping secrets at the alienating parent's behest."

13. Referring to the Targeted Parent by First Name

Definition: "Instead of using "Mommy/Daddy" or "your mommy/your daddy," the alienating parent uses the targeted parent's first name when speaking to the child about them. This can lead the child to do the same. The message conveyed is that the targeted parent is no longer a respected authority figure or someone with a special bond with the child, effectively demoting them to a peer or neighbor."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Yes. There is compelling and undisputed evidence that the children began using the targeted parent's given names or formal titles instead of “Dad” only after their removal from the targeted parent’s care... For example, one child texted, "[Given Name] I do not want to talk to you I’m ending this conversation” and “Sir I’m busy with my friends.” Another child messaged, “Goodnight sir, you may contact me but I will not respond…” and crucially, "[Given Name], you lost the right to call yourself dad a long time ago.”"

14. Referring to a Stepparent as “Mom” or “Dad” and Encouraging Child to do the Same

Definition: "If the alienating parent remarries, they will speak of the new partner as if they are the child's only mother or father. This new partner will be introduced as "mother/father" rather than "stepparent" to others. The alienating parent will refer to them as mother/father to the child and expect the child to do the same, causing hurt and anger to the targeted parent if discovered."

Status: Not Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: No documented instances

Most Egregious Example: Not applicable.

15. Withholding Medical, Academic, and Other Important Information from Targeted Parent/Keeping Targeted Parent’s Name Off Medical, Academic, and Other Relevant Documents

Definition: "The alienating parent will not provide information about the targeted parent on important forms (school, sports, religious education) or may omit it entirely. This disadvantages the targeted parent in accessing information, forming relationships, being contacted in emergencies, participating, and receiving schedule changes. Additionally, the alienating parent will not provide the targeted parent with relevant documents like schedules or notices from school or doctors. These actions marginalize the targeted parent in the eyes of the child and other adults, making it difficult for them to be actively involved."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "The targeted parent itemized multiple “unanswered parenting questions” that they had been asking for over five weeks with no response. These included fundamental concerns such as: “Who watches the children when you are away? What are their current schedules (school pickup/drop-off)? When are their next school and sports events?”"

16. Changing Child’s Name to Remove Association with Targeted Parent

Definition: "If the alienating parent is the mother, she may revert to her maiden name after divorce and use it for her children. If she remarries, she may assume her new husband's surname and use it for her children. If the alienating parent is the father, he might use a new nickname for the child, convincing the child it has always been their name, thereby forging a new identity where the alienating parent is most important. The targeted parent may feel distant and awkward, perceiving the name change as a rejection, leading to hurt, sadness, and frustration."

Status: Not Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: No documented instances

Most Egregious Example: Not applicable.

17. Cultivating Dependency/Undermining the Authority of the Targeted Parent

Definition: "Alienated children often view the alienating parent as perfect and behave dependently in an age-inappropriate way. Alienating parents foster this dependency instead of promoting self-sufficiency and independence. Simultaneously, they undermine the targeted parent's authority to ensure the child's loyalty to only one parent. This includes imposing rules for the child to follow even with the targeted parent and mocking or overriding the targeted parent's rules. This elevates the alienating parent in the child's eyes while diminishing the targeted parent's importance."

Status: Strongly Evidenced

Number of Verified Factual Events: Multiple documented instances

Most Egregious Example: "Their constant questioning of why the targeted parent follows the agreement when it's "not helpful for them" also demonstrates a clear undermining of his adherence to legal and structured parenting. Their desire to leave visitations early to be "more comfortable at home" reinforces their dependency on the alienating parent's environment and a rejection of the targeted parent's space and authority. The oldest child explicitly stated they would "rather be in prison than here at visitations with me," showing extreme resistance and complete disregard for the targeted parent's authority and the visitation process."
